# Seasonal Cold: What Works, and What Doesn't

A viral upper respiratory infection (the common cold) is the most frequent infectious illness. An adult goes through 2–4 episodes per year, a child 6–10. In most cases it's a self-limited condition that does not need antibiotics, "strong" antivirals, or IV drips.

## How it presents

A typical course:

- **Day 1–2:** scratchy throat, nasal congestion, mild weakness.
- **Day 3–5:** symptom peak — runny nose, cough, headache, sometimes a fever up to 38–38.5 °C, body aches.
- **Day 6–10:** gradual improvement. Cough and residual nasal symptoms can linger another 1–2 weeks.

Total duration: 7–10 days. If symptoms worsen after day 5 or return after improvement, a complication is possible — see a clinician.

## What actually helps

There's no medication that shortens a cold by 2–3 days. But there are measures that ease symptoms and support the body while it does the work.

### Rest and sleep

The immune system works more efficiently in sleep. 1–2 days of full rest at the start of the illness often speed recovery more than pushing through.

### Fluids

Drink enough: water, tea, broth, fruit compote. Warm drinks reduce throat discomfort and thin mucus. Light-yellow urine is the marker of adequate hydration.

### Room temperature and humidity

Dry hot air in a heated apartment makes cough and runny nose worse. Keep temperature at 18–20 °C, humidity at 40–60%, and ventilate 2–3 times a day for 10 minutes.

### Nutrition

There are no "superfoods for a cold". Light food, more fruit and vegetables, warm soups — proven classics. Appetite is reduced, that's normal — don't force-feed yourself.

### Symptomatic remedies

- **Paracetamol** or **ibuprofen** — for a fever above 38.5 °C or significant discomfort. Don't suppress 37.3–38 °C — that temperature helps fight the virus.
- **Saline sprays and nasal rinses** — safe, reduce congestion.
- **Decongestant nasal drops** (xylometazoline) — a short 3–5-day course, no longer: they cause rebound congestion.
- **Lozenges and pastilles** — ease sore throat by moistening and mild local anesthesia.
- **Salt-water gargles** — a simple way to ease sore throat.
- **Honey with a warm drink** — reduces nighttime cough in adults and children over 1 year old (no honey for infants under 1 — botulism risk).

## What doesn't work, or works poorly

- **Antibiotics.** A cold is caused by a virus; antibiotics don't affect it. They're needed for bacterial complications (pneumonia, purulent otitis, strep throat) — a clinician makes that diagnosis.
- **Broad-spectrum "antivirals".** Most pharmacy-shelf items lack convincing evidence. Oseltamivir and zanamivir work only for confirmed influenza and only in the first 48 hours.
- **Mega-doses of vitamin C.** Don't shorten cold duration in people with normal nutrition. Regular low doses may slightly reduce frequency in athletes and in cold climates.
- **Echinacea, "immunomodulator" pills.** No reliable evidence of effect.
- **Mustard plasters, cupping, foot baths.** Subjectively soothing perhaps, but don't change the disease course.
- **Hot steam inhalation.** Burn risk outweighs the potential benefit. A nebulizer with saline is safer.

## Prevention

What actually reduces your risk of catching it:

- **Hand-washing** with soap for 20 seconds — the single most effective measure.
- **Avoiding contact with sick people**, especially in enclosed spaces.
- **Annual flu vaccination** — especially for adults over 65, people with chronic conditions, and healthcare workers.
- **Don't touch your face** with unwashed hands — the main route of virus transfer from surfaces.
- **Good sleep, nutrition, physical activity** — keep the immune system functional.
- **COVID-19 vaccination** per current guidance for at-risk groups.

## When to see a doctor

- Temperature **above 39 °C**, especially lasting more than 3 days.
- **Shortness of breath**, chest pain, inability to take a full breath.
- **Symptoms worsening after day 5** or fever returning after improvement.
- **Severe headache** with light sensitivity and vomiting.
- Purulent nasal discharge for more than 10 days.
- Ear pain, especially in children.
- In infants under 3 months — any fever above 38 °C.
- Dehydration (dry mouth, low urine output, lethargy in a child).
- Chronic lung or heart disease, diabetes, immune deficiency — see a clinician earlier.

## When to call emergency services

- Bluish lips or fingers.
- Pronounced shortness of breath at rest.
- Inability to drink or eat, signs of dehydration in a child.
- Confusion, seizures.
- A rash that doesn't fade under pressure.
