# Adult Health Screenings: What to Check, and When, by Age

Screenings are tests and exams in people without symptoms — to catch disease while it's easier to treat. Not all screenings are equally useful: some reduce mortality and genuinely work, others create false alarms and unnecessary procedures. Below is a rough guide based on WHO, USPSTF, and national-program recommendations for average-risk adults. Your individual list may differ.

## 20–39 years

**Annually or every 2–3 years:**

- **Blood pressure** — even for young adults, at least every 2 years. One in five 30-year-olds has elevated BP without symptoms yet.
- **BMI and waist circumference** — basic anthropometry at any visit.
- **Depression and anxiety screening** — short questionnaires (PHQ-2, GAD-2) with your primary care provider.
- **Dentist** — at least once a year.
- **Dermatologist** — if you have many moles, a family history of melanoma, or high regular sun exposure.

**By indication:**

- **Lipid panel** — first check around 25–30; earlier with obesity, family diabetes, or known hypertension.
- **HbA1c or fasting glucose** — with obesity, hypertension, family history of diabetes.
- **Cervical screening (Pap test, HPV).** From age 21; schedule depends on prior results — every 3 to 5 years.
- **STI screening** — with new partners, unprotected sex — HIV, syphilis, chlamydia, gonorrhea.

## 40–49 years

Added to the above:

- **Lipid panel** — every 3–5 years even without risk factors.
- **HbA1c** — every 3 years as baseline.
- **Mammography.** Start age 40–45 for women is a shared decision — individual risk, breast density, family history. Some protocols start at 50.
- **Colorectal screening.** Per current USPSTF guidance, starts at 45: colonoscopy every 10 years, or annual stool-based FIT as an alternative.
- **Glaucoma and visual acuity** — eye exam every 2–4 years, even if vision seems unchanged.

## 50–64 years

- **Colonoscopy or FIT** — continuing.
- **Mammography** — every 1–2 years.
- **Low-dose lung CT** — for a 20-pack-year smoking history; annually from 50 to 80.
- **Bone density (DEXA)** in postmenopausal women by 65 (earlier with risk factors: low weight, prior fractures, corticosteroids, early menopause).
- **Blood pressure, lipids, HbA1c** — more often (every 1–2 years) as cardiovascular risk rises.
- **Vaccines:** zoster after 50, pneumococcal per schedule.

## 65+ years

- **Sensory function:** hearing and vision — every 1–2 years.
- **Cognitive function.** A brief screen (e.g. Mini-Cog) at primary-care visits — especially when family members raise concerns.
- **Bone density** every 2 years.
- **Fall risk.** Gait assessment, home safety review (rugs, lighting, bathroom grab bars).
- **Vaccines:** annual influenza, RSV, pneumococcal, COVID-19 per current guidance.

## Not part of routine screening

This doesn't mean "never"; it means — absent symptoms or risk factors, routine screening is not indicated:

- **Whole-body MRI/CT "to check"** — CT radiation and a high false-positive rate.
- **Tumor markers** (CA-125, CEA, PSA after 70 in asymptomatic people) — a case-by-case discussion with a clinician, not mass screening.
- **Resting ECG** in a healthy asymptomatic adult — doesn't reduce mortality, often cascades into unnecessary workups.
- **Parasite panels** without symptoms, "body cleanse" packages.

## When to see a doctor off-schedule

- Any **new symptom** that doesn't resolve in 2–3 weeks.
- **Unexplained weight loss** (more than 4–5% of body weight over 6 months).
- **Blood in stool or urine**, changes in bowel habits, difficulty swallowing.
- **Non-healing sores, unusual lumps**, a changing mole.
- **Chronic fatigue** not tied to lifestyle.

## The main point

The best screening is the one you actually get. A perfect long list forgotten after one visit helps no one. A short, regular one does.
