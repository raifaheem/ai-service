# Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis): How to Calm the Skin and Keep Flares Away

Atopic dermatitis is a chronic inflammatory skin condition affecting 10–20% of children and 2–5% of adults. It's not an infection and not contagious, but treating the skin "like it's infected" is a common mistake that makes things worse. The goal isn't to cure it once and for all — it's to get the skin into remission and keep it there.

## What it looks like

Classic signs:

- Dry, itchy skin — especially in the bends of elbows, behind the knees, on the neck, and on the face in infants.
- Reddened, scaly patches, sometimes with weeping cracks after scratching.
- Thickened, rough skin where flares are chronic (lichenification).
- A wave-like pattern: flares alternate with quieter periods.

Itch is usually worst in the evening and at night. "Scratch — relief — worse an hour later" is the typical loop; controlling the itch matters more than it seems.

## What triggers flares

- **Dry air**, especially in heated apartments in winter (humidity often drops to 20–30% when 40–60% is ideal).
- **Hot water**, high-pH soap, harsh shower gels.
- **Sweat, wool, coarsely woven synthetic fabrics.**
- **Emotional stress**, poor sleep.
- **Food triggers** in some children (most commonly milk, egg, nuts). In adults they're much less significant than assumed — blanket food elimination isn't needed.
- **House dust, pollen, pet dander** — especially with coexisting atopy.
- **Scratching itself** — causes microtrauma, inflammation, and more itch (the itch-scratch cycle).

## Daily skincare

Atopic skin loses water faster than normal skin. Restoring the barrier is the foundation:

- **Short showers, 5–10 minutes, lukewarm (not hot) water.** Soap only as a mild pH-neutral syndet, locally, not every day.
- **Emollient within 3 minutes of showering**, while skin is still damp. Generously — twice daily during flares, once in remission.
- **Humidifier** in the bedroom in winter, aiming for 40–50% relative humidity.
- Cotton or soft synthetic blends; pre-wash new clothes before wearing.
- Keep nails short, and use cotton gloves at night for children who scratch in their sleep.

## What helps during a flare

- **A short course of topical corticosteroids** as prescribed — this is the backbone of treatment. Strength and duration are individualized; steroid phobia often leads to undertreated flares and longer courses later.
- **Calcineurin inhibitors** (topical tacrolimus, pimecrolimus) — a non-steroid option for face and skin folds where steroids need caution.
- **Oral antihistamines** don't directly stop the itch but can help with sleep during severe nighttime itch.
- **Wet wrapping** for severe pediatric flares — only under clinical guidance.

## When to see a doctor

- If a flare doesn't respond to home care within 7–10 days.
- If itch is disrupting sleep or work.
- If your usual routine stops working, emollients "don't hold."
- On a first adult episode with no clear cause.
- For children — annual dermatology review to adjust the plan, even when things look calm.

## Red flags — get urgent care

Signs of infected eczema that need prompt medical evaluation:

- **Yellow crusts, pus**, pustules, a smell from the patch — bacterial infection (usually Staphylococcus).
- **Grouped blisters with fever** — possible eczema herpeticum, a dangerous herpes-virus complication.
- **Rapidly spreading redness** beyond the usual patches, with fever.

These need prescriptions (antibiotics, antivirals) — self-treatment tends to make them worse.
