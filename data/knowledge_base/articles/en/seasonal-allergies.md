# Seasonal Allergies: Managing Hay Fever

Seasonal allergic rhinitis ("hay fever") affects up to 20–30% of adults. Symptoms mimic a cold but last weeks or months, don't come with fever, and repeat every year in the same season.

## How to tell allergy from a cold

| Feature | Cold | Allergy |
|---|---|---|
| Duration | 7–10 days | weeks, as long as the allergen is present |
| Fever | occasional, 37–38 °C | no |
| Nasal discharge | yellow-green after day 2–3 | clear, watery |
| Eye itching | rare | typical |
| Sore throat | typical | uncommon, usually post-nasal drip |
| Seasonality | any time of year | same time every year |

## Main seasonal triggers

- **Spring (April–May)**: tree pollen (birch, alder, hazel, oak).
- **Summer (June–August)**: grass pollen (timothy, meadow fescue, rye).
- **Late summer — autumn (August–October)**: weed pollen (ragweed, mugwort).
- **Year-round** — dust mites, mold, pet dander — technically not "seasonal", but symptoms often worsen in certain seasons.

The specific trigger matters: knowing you're allergic to birch means different preventive windows than knowing you're allergic to ragweed.

## Symptoms

- Sneezing, often in bursts.
- Clear, watery nasal discharge.
- Itching in the nose, palate, eyes, ears.
- Nasal congestion, especially at night.
- Red, watery eyes (allergic conjunctivitis).
- Fatigue, concentration problems from poor sleep and antihistamine side effects.

Severe or unusual symptoms — facial swelling, difficulty breathing, widespread rash — require emergency care; these are signs of anaphylaxis, not simple hay fever.

## Reducing exposure

Can't eliminate pollen, but can significantly lower the dose:

- **Check pollen forecasts** and plan outdoor activity on low-pollen days.
- **Peak pollen hours**: 5–10 AM and late afternoon. Open windows in other hours.
- **After outdoor time**: change clothes, wash hands and face, rinse sinuses with saline.
- **Wash hair before bed** so pollen doesn't end up on your pillow.
- **HEPA filter** in the bedroom or on the AC reduces indoor pollen.
- **Don't dry laundry outside** during high pollen counts.
- **Wear sunglasses outside** — reduces eye exposure.
- **Shower before sleep**.

## Medication treatment

### First-line: non-sedating antihistamines

Cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine — OTC, once daily. Work within 1–2 hours. Less drowsy than older antihistamines (diphenhydramine, chlorpheniramine), which are no longer recommended for chronic use.

### Intranasal corticosteroids

Fluticasone, mometasone, budesonide sprays — the most effective treatment for moderate-to-severe allergic rhinitis. Key points:

- Full effect builds over 1–2 weeks of regular use.
- Start 1–2 weeks **before** expected pollen season for maximum effect.
- Safe for long-term seasonal use at recommended doses.
- Correct technique: aim the spray toward the outer wall of the nose, not the septum.

### Combination therapy

For severe symptoms: intranasal steroid + antihistamine. More effective than either alone.

### Short-term use

- **Decongestant nasal sprays** (oxymetazoline, xylometazoline) — no more than 3–5 days. Longer use causes rebound congestion.
- **Eye drops with antihistamine** — for prominent eye symptoms.
- **Oral decongestants** (pseudoephedrine) — raise blood pressure and heart rate. Avoid with hypertension, heart disease, in elderly.

### What to avoid

- Long-term use of sedating antihistamines (diphenhydramine) — side effects outweigh benefits.
- Decongestant sprays beyond 5 days.
- "Natural" remedies without evidence (most herbal preparations).

## Allergen immunotherapy (ASIT)

The only treatment that affects the underlying mechanism, not just symptoms. Indications:

- Moderate-to-severe symptoms not controlled by medication.
- Symptoms 3+ months of the year.
- Clear correlation between symptoms and a specific allergen.

Two forms:

- **Subcutaneous injections** (SCIT) — given in-clinic, 3–5 year course.
- **Sublingual tablets or drops** (SLIT) — taken at home, same duration.

Effect builds over a year; long-term benefit can persist for years after completing treatment. Effectiveness is highest for pollen and dust mite allergies.

## When to see a doctor

- Symptoms interfere with sleep or daytime activities despite OTC treatment.
- Need for OTC medications more than 3 months of the year.
- Suspected asthma (wheezing, dry cough, especially at night or during exercise) — allergic rhinitis and asthma often coexist.
- Sinus infections 2+ times a season.
- To evaluate immunotherapy suitability.

## Red flags — emergency care

- Facial, lip, tongue, or throat swelling.
- Shortness of breath, wheezing.
- Generalized itching or rash, especially with weakness and dizziness.
- Loss of consciousness after an insect sting or new medication.

These are signs of anaphylaxis, requiring immediate injection of epinephrine and emergency care.
