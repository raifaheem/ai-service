# Hydration: How Much to Drink and When It Actually Matters

The advice "drink 8 glasses of water a day" is a simplification that ignores weight, climate, activity level, and the fact that we get a significant portion of fluid from food. That said, adequate hydration really does affect well-being, concentration, physical performance, and even the frequency of headaches.

## How much water you actually need

The European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) gives the following adequate intake levels (from drinks and food combined):

- Men: about **2.5 L/day**.
- Women: about **2.0 L/day**.
- Children 4–8 years: 1.6 L.
- Pregnancy: +300 ml over usual intake.
- Lactation: +700 ml.

About 20–30% of this comes from food (soups, vegetables, fruits, yogurt); the rest from drinks: water, tea, coffee, milk, juice.

A practical rule of thumb: **30–35 ml of water per kg of body weight per day** for an adult in a temperate climate without intense activity.

## What increases your needs

- **Heat and humidity** — sweat losses reach 1–2 L per hour.
- **Physical activity** — moderate exercise adds 0.5–1 L, intense workouts more.
- **Fever** — each degree above 37 °C / 98.6 °F raises needs by about 10%.
- **Vomiting and diarrhea** — the main cause of dehydration; acute GI infections call for oral rehydration solutions.
- **Dry air** (winter heating, air travel) — accelerates fluid loss through skin and breath.
- **Breastfeeding** — an extra 700 ml per day.

## Signs of dehydration

Mild to moderate dehydration:

- Dry mouth, thirst.
- Dark yellow urine, reduced volume.
- Headache, fatigue, poor concentration.
- Dry skin; a pinched skin fold on the back of the hand is slow to flatten.

Severe dehydration (**needs medical care**):

- Confusion, drowsiness.
- Rapid heart rate, low blood pressure.
- No urine for more than 8 hours.
- In children: sunken eyes, no tears when crying, sunken fontanelle in infants.

## The simplest test — urine color

The most accessible indicator. Pale yellow or straw-colored is normal. Dark yellow or amber — drink more. Clear like water usually means you're drinking plenty; for most people this isn't harmful, but excess intake brings no extra benefits.

Exception: riboflavin (vitamin B2) supplements or beet juice can color urine regardless of hydration.

## Water myths

- **"Coffee and tea dehydrate you."** Myth. Moderate caffeine doses (up to 400 mg/day) have a mild diuretic effect that's offset by the volume of fluid consumed. Coffee and tea count toward your total intake.
- **"You need 8 glasses of pure water on top of food."** Myth. The recommendation includes all fluids, including from soups, fruits, and milk.
- **"Thirst means you're already dehydrated."** Slightly outdated. Mild thirst is a physiological signal — responding to it is normal. Be concerned if you don't feel thirst despite obvious dryness, which happens in older adults.
- **"More is always better."** Not true. Water intoxication (hyponatremia) is seen in marathon runners and in people with psychiatric disorders who drink 5–10 L per day without salt replacement. Dangerous but rare.

## What to drink

- **Water** — the base. Tap water (in countries with good quality control) or bottled — the difference is minor.
- **Tea, coffee without sugar** — count toward fluid intake.
- **Milk, plant milks** — a good source of fluid, especially around workouts.
- **Juice, soda** — contain sugar. Limit to 150 ml per day.
- **Sports drinks** — make sense for workouts over an hour or in the heat; in daily life they're extra sugar.
- **Alcohol** — increases urine output, does not count toward fluid balance.

## Simple habits

- Keep a bottle of water on your desk — visibility raises intake.
- Tie drinking to routines: a glass after waking up, before each meal, before exercise.
- In the heat or during exercise, drink small amounts every 15–20 minutes — don't wait for strong thirst.
- Fruits and vegetables high in water (cucumber, watermelon, oranges) are a pleasant and nutritious way to top up.

## Special groups

- **Older adults** often drink too little because thirst sensation declines. It helps to drink on a schedule.
- **Children** don't judge thirst well; offer water regularly, especially in heat.
- **People with heart or kidney failure** should coordinate fluid volume with a doctor — excess can worsen edema.
