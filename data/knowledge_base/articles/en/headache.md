# Headache: Causes, Self-Help, and Warning Signs

Headache is one of the most common reasons people consult a general practitioner. The vast majority of cases are **benign** and resolve without specific treatment, but it's important to be able to tell an ordinary headache apart from the forms that call for prompt medical attention.

## Main types of primary headache

**Tension-type headache** is by far the most common. It feels like a pressing band or heaviness around the head, usually on both sides, and is rarely very intense. Typical triggers: long hours at a computer, an awkward posture, poor sleep, emotional stress, dehydration, or skipped meals.

**Migraine** is a throbbing pain, more often on one side, ranging from moderate to severe. It is often accompanied by nausea, vomiting, and heightened sensitivity to light and sound. An attack can last from a few hours to several days. Some people experience an "aura" before the headache — visual or sensory disturbances such as flickering lights, blind spots, or numbness in the face or arm.

**Cluster headache** is less common but very intense: a piercing pain around one eye, with tearing, a stuffy nose, and redness in the eye on the same side. Attacks come in series, often at predictable times of day.

## What you can do on your own

For mild to moderate tension-type headaches, non-drug measures are usually enough:

- Drink a glass of water. A frequent reason for a mild headache is plain dehydration.
- Take a break from the screen — the "20-20-20" rule: every 20 minutes, look at something about 20 feet (6 meters) away for 20 seconds.
- Ventilate the room or take a short walk outside.
- Try diaphragmatic breathing: 4 seconds in, 6 seconds out, for about 5 minutes.
- If possible, a short 20–30 minute nap can help.

Over-the-counter analgesics (paracetamol, ibuprofen) can be effective, but using them more than **10 days per month** is not recommended: that itself can cause "medication-overuse" headache, which is often more stubborn than the original problem.

## When to make a routine doctor's appointment

Schedule a non-urgent visit if:

- Headaches have become frequent — more than 1–2 times a week — or any single episode lasts longer than 72 hours.
- Your usual over-the-counter remedies have stopped helping.
- The pain interferes with work, study, or daily life.
- You suspect migraines and want to discuss prevention. Effective preventive treatments exist; they only work when started under a clinician's supervision.

## Red flags — seek emergency care immediately

Any of the following calls for urgent medical evaluation:

- **A sudden "thunderclap" headache** that reaches peak intensity within seconds, as if struck.
- The **first-ever episode of a severe headache after age 50.**
- Headache with **fever, neck stiffness, or extreme sensitivity to light.**
- Headache accompanied by **confusion, weakness in an arm or leg, slurred speech, or vision changes.**
- Headache **after a head injury**, especially if it gets worse over hours or days.
- Headache that wakes you from sleep or sharply worsens with coughing, bending forward, or straining.
- Headache during pregnancy or shortly after delivery, especially with high blood pressure or swelling.

These signs may indicate serious conditions — stroke, subarachnoid hemorrhage, meningitis, increased intracranial pressure — and require immediate diagnostic workup.

## Common migraine triggers worth tracking

Migraine triggers vary widely between people. The following list comes up most often, but yours may differ:

- Skipped meals or long gaps without eating.
- Disrupted sleep — both too little and too much.
- Specific foods (aged cheese, processed meats, alcohol — especially red wine).
- Hormonal changes around the menstrual cycle.
- Bright or flickering lights, strong smells.
- Weather shifts, especially rapid changes in atmospheric pressure.
- Stress, including the "let-down" period after a stressful event.

Identifying your personal triggers is more useful than following a generic "avoid everything" list — most people can keep two or three habitual triggers in mind and don't need to restructure their whole diet.

## Keeping a headache diary

If headaches have become regular, a simple diary is one of the most useful diagnostic tools your clinician has. Record:

- Date, start time, and end time.
- Intensity on a 0–10 scale.
- Possible triggers (stress, food, menstrual cycle, weather, sleep quality).
- What you tried, and whether it helped.

Two to four weeks of diary entries can shorten the path to the right diagnosis and treatment considerably.

## Myths about headaches

- **"A bad headache always means something serious is wrong with my brain."** The vast majority of headaches — even severe migraines — are primary headaches with no structural disease behind them. Red flags (above) are what point toward something serious.
- **"I should push through and keep taking painkillers."** Frequent over-the-counter painkiller use can convert occasional headaches into a near-daily problem (medication-overuse headache). If you need them more than 10 days a month, see a doctor.
- **"Only sufferers of migraines need preventive treatment."** Even chronic tension-type headache can be addressed with non-drug approaches (sleep regularity, physical activity, stress management, sometimes physiotherapy) — and these often work without any medication at all.
