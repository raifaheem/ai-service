# Type 2 Diabetes: How to Lower Your Risk and What to Do About Prediabetes

Type 2 diabetes isn't really about "too much sugar" — it's about the body becoming less responsive to insulin. The condition has a long precursor stage called prediabetes, during which the process is still reversible: CDC data on the Diabetes Prevention Program shows a **58% reduction** in progression to diabetes through lifestyle change. It is one of the few chronic diseases where prevention genuinely works.

## Who is at risk

Factors that raise your risk:

- BMI of 25 kg/m² or higher (23+ for many Asian populations).
- Waist circumference over 94 cm (men) or 80 cm (women).
- Sedentary lifestyle.
- Hypertension, elevated cholesterol.
- Family history of diabetes (parents, siblings).
- Age over 45.
- History of gestational diabetes or giving birth to a baby over 4 kg.

Two or more factors is reason to get tested even without symptoms.

## Useful tests

- **Fasting glucose.** Normal up to 5.5 mmol/L (100 mg/dL). 5.6–6.9 (100–125) — prediabetes. 7.0 (126) or higher on a repeat — diabetes.
- **HbA1c (glycated hemoglobin).** Reflects average blood sugar over the past 2–3 months. Normal up to 5.7%. 5.7–6.4% — prediabetes. 6.5% and above — diabetes.
- **Oral glucose tolerance test** — used for borderline values and in pregnancy.

A single glucose measurement is a weak test. If borderline, a clinician usually repeats or adds HbA1c.

## Early signs

Type 2 diabetes often stays silent for a long time. Watch for:

- Unusual thirst, frequent urination — especially at night.
- Fatigue, sleepiness after meals.
- Slow wound healing, frequent skin infections.
- Itching (especially groin; yeast infections in women).
- Blurry vision that fluctuates day to day.
- Darkened, velvety skin in neck or armpit folds — acanthosis nigricans, a common marker of insulin resistance.

Any of these alongside risk factors is a reason to get tested, not to wait.

## What works for prevention

Large trials (DPP in the U.S., Finnish DPS) consistently show a 50–60% risk reduction from three elements combined.

- **5–7% weight loss.** For a 90 kg adult that's 5–6 kg. Not "lose 20 by summer" — sustained, modest loss.
- **150 minutes of physical activity per week.** Moderate aerobic (brisk walking, swimming, cycling) plus 2 strength sessions. Working muscles pull glucose out of blood more aggressively — this helps even without weight loss.
- **Diet changes.** More vegetables, whole grains, legumes, fish. Less sugary drinks, white-flour baked goods, processed meat. Mediterranean and DASH patterns show the strongest results.

## On sugar and diet

- Total carbohydrate restriction isn't required and often backfires. The issue is fast carbs (sweets, white bread, soda), not oats and fruit.
- Sugary drinks are the single strongest risk factor. One daily glass of soda or juice raises diabetes risk by 10–15%.
- "Diabetic" cookies made with fructose aren't safe — fructose in large amounts stresses the liver and promotes insulin resistance.

## When to see a doctor

- With prediabetes — HbA1c check every 6–12 months plus lifestyle work.
- With diagnosed diabetes — individualized follow-up: endocrinologist, annual eye exam (retina), kidney (urine) and foot checks.
- In pregnancy — diabetes screening at 24–28 weeks.

## Red flags — emergency care

Signs of diabetic decompensation needing urgent care:

- **Fruity/acetone breath**, nausea, vomiting, severe weakness — possible diabetic ketoacidosis.
- **Deep drowsiness, confusion, dry skin, rapid deep breathing.**
- **Loss of consciousness** in someone with diabetes is always an emergency.
- **A non-healing foot wound**, especially red or draining — risk of diabetic foot infection.
