# Vitamin D: What It's For, How to Get It, Who Should Test

Vitamin D isn't a vitamin in the classic sense — it's more of a hormone the body synthesizes itself under ultraviolet light. Deficiency in countries above the 40th parallel is widespread in winter.

## What vitamin D does in the body

Its main role is regulating calcium and phosphorus metabolism — and therefore bone and tooth health. Without adequate vitamin D, calcium absorption from food drops by 60–70%, no matter how much dairy you eat.

Beyond bones, vitamin D receptors are found in muscle, immune cells, and nervous tissue. So deficiency is linked to muscle weakness, increased fall risk in older adults, more frequent colds, and seasonal mood shifts.

## How much you need

Generally accepted reference values (EFSA, IOM):

- Adults 18–70: **600–800 IU per day** (15–20 µg).
- Over 70: **800–1000 IU**.
- Pregnant and breastfeeding people: **600–800 IU**.
- Safe upper limit for adults without clinical supervision: **4000 IU per day**.

Target blood level (25-OH vitamin D): above 30 ng/ml (75 nmol/L). Below 20 ng/ml is deficiency.

## Sources

**Sun.** Under UVB light, the skin converts cholesterol to vitamin D3. About 15–20 minutes of sun exposure (face and forearms) 2–3 times a week in summer is enough. Important caveats: in northern latitudes UVB is insufficient even at noon in winter; SPF 30 sunscreen blocks up to 95% of synthesis; vitamin D doesn't form through glass.

**Food.** A fairly limited list:

- Fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines) — 400–600 IU per 100 g.
- Egg yolk — about 40 IU.
- Cod liver.
- Fortified foods (milk, cereals, margarine) — typically 100–200 IU per serving.

Getting the daily target from an ordinary diet is hard: you'd need fatty fish every day or a supplement.

**Supplements.** Cholecalciferol (D3) is preferable to ergocalciferol (D2): it raises blood levels more effectively. Daily (1000–2000 IU) or weekly (7000 IU once a week) are equivalent. Best absorbed with a fatty meal.

## Who should test

Routine mass screening isn't recommended by most international clinical associations, but it makes sense in these situations:

- Symptoms suggesting deficiency: muscle weakness, bone pain, frequent fractures, fatigue that doesn't lift with rest.
- Risk factors: age over 65, dark skin, consistent full-cover clothing, indoor work without windows, living far north.
- Chronic GI disease (celiac, inflammatory bowel disease, post-gastric resection), chronic kidney or liver disease.
- Long-term anticonvulsants or glucocorticoid therapy.
- Obesity — vitamin D is "stored" in fat tissue and less available.
- Osteoporosis or osteopenia on bone density scan.

## Correcting a deficiency

For confirmed deficiency (25-OH-D below 20 ng/ml), a clinician may prescribe a loading dose: 50 000 IU weekly for 6–8 weeks, then maintenance 1000–2000 IU/day with a re-test in 3 months.

Don't self-prescribe mega-doses (10 000 IU/day or more long-term). Toxicity is possible: nausea, weakness, elevated blood calcium, calcium deposits in the kidneys.

## Common misconceptions

- **"Vitamin D for everything."** There's no convincing evidence that vitamin D supplementation prevents cancer, cardiovascular disease, or depression in people without deficiency. Its value is primarily for bone and muscle systems.
- **"More is better."** Above 50 ng/ml no additional benefit is seen, and chronic excess is toxic.
- **"Tanning beds replace sun."** Most tanning beds emit primarily UVA, which barely stimulates vitamin D synthesis but raises skin cancer risk.
