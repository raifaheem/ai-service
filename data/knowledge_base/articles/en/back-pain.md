# Back Pain: What to Do and When to See a Doctor

Low back pain is one of the most common reasons for short-term disability in adults. Up to 80% of people experience at least one episode of back pain in their lifetime. The good news: most cases are **non-specific back pain** — not caused by serious disease and resolving on their own within 4–6 weeks.

## Common causes of non-specific back pain

- Muscle strain from unfamiliar physical activity.
- Prolonged static posture — sitting at a computer, driving long distances.
- Awkward movement combining rotation and bending.
- Emotional stress maintaining elevated muscle tension.
- Poor sleep or an unsupportive mattress.

It helps to know: MRI findings in people **without any back pain** commonly show "abnormalities" — disc protrusions, small herniations, degenerative changes. These correlate with age, not necessarily with pain. That's why routine MRI for typical back pain without red flags is not recommended.

## What to do in the first few days

Modern guidelines (NICE, WHO) are the opposite of the old "stay in bed and don't move" advice:

- **Stay active** within your tolerance. Bed rest slows recovery.
- Continue your usual activities, avoiding only those that clearly worsen the pain.
- **Heat therapy** — a heating pad or warm shower — helps relieve muscle spasm.
- Over-the-counter pain relievers (paracetamol, ibuprofen) are reasonable for a short 3–5 day course.
- Keep walking: a light 15–20 minute walk several times a day often helps more than lying down.

## Managing recurring pain

If episodes of back pain keep returning, regular physical activity and ergonomics matter most:

- **Core strengthening exercises** — planks, cat-cow stretches, glute bridges. 10–15 minutes every other day.
- **Stretching** of hamstrings and hip flexors — they directly affect the load on your lower back.
- **Aerobic activity** of moderate intensity (brisk walking, swimming, cycling) for 150 minutes per week.
- **Workplace ergonomics**: feet flat on the floor, lower back supported, monitor at eye level, a movement break every 45–60 minutes.
- Working with a psychologist or learning stress management techniques plays a key role in chronic pain.

## Red flags: when to seek medical care quickly

Seek prompt medical attention if back pain is accompanied by:

- **Loss of bladder or bowel control**, numbness in the groin area — signs of cauda equina syndrome, which requires emergency intervention.
- **Progressive leg weakness**, inability to stand on your foot.
- Pain **after significant trauma** (fall, car accident).
- Night pain that doesn't ease with rest and wakes you from sleep.
- Unexplained **weight loss**, night sweats, fever.
- Pain in someone with a history of cancer or long-term corticosteroid use.

Schedule a non-urgent visit if pain doesn't improve over 4–6 weeks despite staying active and using over-the-counter measures, or if it significantly disrupts daily life.

## Myths about back pain

- **"A disc herniation requires surgery."** Surgery is indicated only in a small percentage of cases with progressive neurological symptoms. Most herniations resolve with conservative care.
- **"You need an orthopedic mattress."** A medium-firm mattress suits most people. Overall activity level and comfortable sleeping posture matter more.
- **"A back brace protects your spine."** Long-term brace wear weakens your own muscles. Use short-term during acute pain only.

## Practical prevention

- Lift properly: bend at the hips and knees, keep the load close to your body, don't twist while lifting.
- If you sit all day, take movement breaks and vary your posture.
- Stay at a healthy weight — excess abdominal weight shifts your center of gravity and strains the lower back.
- Quit smoking — it reduces blood flow to discs and delays healing.
- Manage stress — chronic tension contributes to muscle pain.
