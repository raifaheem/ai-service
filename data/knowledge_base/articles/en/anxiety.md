# Anxiety: When It's Normal and When It's Time to Get Help

Anxiety is a normal emotion. Feeling nervous before a job interview, exam, or important conversation is natural. The problem starts when anxiety appears without a clear cause, lasts for weeks, interferes with daily life, or shows up as panic attacks.

## Distinguishing normal anxiety from a disorder

**Normal anxiety** is tied to a specific event, ends when the event passes, and mobilizes you to act.

**An anxiety disorder** is a different category:

- Lasts **more than 6 months** (for generalized anxiety disorder).
- Disproportionate to real threat, or present without a specific cause.
- Accompanied by physical symptoms: rapid heartbeat, sweating, trembling, shortness of breath, "lump in the throat", GI upset, sleep disturbance.
- Interferes with work, study, or relationships.

According to WHO, anxiety disorders are the most common mental health conditions worldwide, affecting around 4% of the population at any time. This isn't "character" or "weakness" — it's a condition with biological underpinnings and effective treatment.

## Common forms

- **Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD)** — persistent low-grade worry about many areas of life, hard to control.
- **Panic disorder** — sudden intense episodes of anxiety with physical symptoms (palpitations, shortness of breath, fear of dying), lasting 5–30 minutes.
- **Social anxiety disorder** — fear of judgment by others, avoidance of public situations.
- **Specific phobias** — fear of heights, flying, animals, enclosed spaces.
- **Agoraphobia** — fear of situations that are hard to leave (transit, crowds).

## Panic attack: what it is and what to do

A panic attack is an intense surge of anxiety with pronounced physical symptoms. It's **frightening, but not dangerous** — it doesn't cause death, loss of consciousness, or a heart attack.

During an attack:

1. **Sit down and slow your breathing.** A longer exhale (4 seconds in, 6–8 seconds out) calms the nervous system.
2. **The "5-4-3-2-1" technique**: name 5 things you see, 4 you hear, 3 you feel on your skin, 2 smells, 1 taste. This returns attention to the present moment.
3. **Say to yourself**: "This is a panic attack. It's unpleasant, but it will end in 10–20 minutes. I'm safe."
4. **Don't flee** the situation if possible — fleeing reinforces the fear and increases the chance of recurrence.

## What works long term

### Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT)

First-line treatment for anxiety disorders. It targets:

- **Thoughts** — learning to recognize distortions ("catastrophizing", "mind reading", black-and-white thinking) and replace them with realistic alternatives.
- **Behavior** — gradual exposure to feared situations reduces anxiety intensity.
- **Skills** — breathing, muscle relaxation, attention training.

CBT's effectiveness for anxiety disorders is comparable to medication, and in some forms superior. A typical course is 10–20 sessions.

### Medications

Prescribed by a psychiatrist or physician. Main groups:

- **SSRI antidepressants** (sertraline, escitalopram) — first line for GAD, panic disorder, social phobia. Effect builds over 4–8 weeks.
- **Benzodiazepines** — quick relief of acute anxiety but cause dependence with prolonged use. Short courses only.
- **Beta-blockers** — helpful for situational anxiety (public speaking).

Medications aren't a "hard drug that will dull your personality". They're a tool to help you return to a functional state from which you can work on underlying causes.

### Self-help

Works alongside therapy, and for mild anxiety may be enough on its own:

- **Regular physical activity** — one of the strongest natural anxiolytics.
- **Limiting caffeine and alcohol** — both substances increase anxiety, though by different mechanisms.
- **7–9 hours of sleep** — sleep deprivation worsens anxiety.
- **Mindfulness meditation** — 10–20 minutes per day lowers baseline anxiety.
- **Worry journal** — writing down anxious thoughts reduces their intensity.
- **Limiting news consumption** — especially negative information flow before bed.

## What not to do

- **Avoid** feared situations. Short-term relief but long-term strengthening of the fear.
- **Self-medicate with alcohol** — relieves anxiety for 1–2 hours, worsens it for the next 12–24.
- **Supplements "for nerves"** without evidence. Valerian and melatonin may slightly help sleep but don't treat an anxiety disorder.
- **Wait for it to "pass on its own"** when symptoms are severe and last more than a month.

## When to see a specialist

Reach out to a psychiatrist, therapist, or primary care doctor if:

- Anxiety lasts more than 4 weeks and disrupts work, study, or relationships.
- You're avoiding normal activities due to fear.
- Panic attacks have appeared.
- There are disturbances in sleep, appetite, or persistent low mood.
- You're using alcohol or medications to cope with anxiety.
- Thoughts of self-harm arise — **this requires urgent care**.

Anxiety disorders respond well to treatment. Most people see substantial improvement within 2–4 months through a combination of therapy and self-help.
