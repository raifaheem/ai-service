# Heartburn and GERD: When It's Lifestyle, When It's a Condition

Around 20–30% of adults get heartburn at least occasionally. Most episodes are a reaction to a big meal or a late dinner. When heartburn returns more than twice a week, it's gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) — a condition worth treating before it gets complicated.

## Why heartburn happens

Your stomach produces hydrochloric acid — that's normal. The lower esophageal sphincter is supposed to keep stomach contents down there. Heartburn happens when that sphincter relaxes briefly and acid rises into the esophagus, whose lining isn't built to handle acid.

What promotes reflux:

- Excess weight, especially abdominal (visceral) fat.
- Large portions, eating before bed, nighttime snacking.
- Lying down right after eating.
- Smoking, alcohol, strong coffee, carbonated drinks.
- Certain foods: spicy, fatty, chocolate, mint, tomatoes, citrus — each person has their own list.
- Pregnancy, especially second and third trimesters.
- Some medications — NSAIDs, some blood-pressure drugs, bisphosphonates.

## What you can do yourself

If heartburn is occasional, simple steps help:

- **Finish dinner 3 hours before bed**; smaller portions.
- **Raise the head of the bed by 10–15 cm** — prop the mattress, not add an extra pillow. Sleeping on your left side reduces reflux more than the right.
- **Lose weight.** Even 5–10% of body weight meaningfully reduces symptoms in people who are overweight.
- **Quitting smoking** gives noticeable improvement over 2–3 months.
- **Limit alcohol and coffee**, especially in the evening.
- Keep a brief food-and-symptom diary for 2–3 weeks — the real triggers are often different from the ones you suspected.

Over-the-counter antacids (magnesium-, calcium-, aluminum-based) handle the occasional episode. Proton pump inhibitors are stronger, but taking them beyond 2 weeks without a doctor isn't recommended — chronic unsupervised use is associated with B12 and magnesium deficiency, higher fracture risk, and more gut infections.

## When to see a doctor

Schedule a visit with a primary care doctor or gastroenterologist if:

- Heartburn more than twice a week for a month.
- Antacids help less and less, or you need them daily.
- Symptoms start for the first time after age 55.
- Persistent cough or voice hoarseness, especially in the morning.

A clinician may recommend a treatment course and, if warranted, an endoscopy to rule out erosive esophagitis or Barrett's esophagus.

## Red flags — seek care promptly

Emergency or urgent signs:

- **Difficulty or pain swallowing** — food feels like it gets stuck.
- **Vomiting blood** or material that looks like coffee grounds, black tarry stool — signs of GI bleeding.
- **Unexplained weight loss**, persistent weakness, anemia.
- **Chest pain**, especially pressure-like, radiating to arm, jaw, or back, with shortness of breath — can mimic a heart attack. When in doubt, seek emergency care rather than wait.

## What not to worry about

- A glass of water, milk, or some yogurt often calms an episode better than baking soda — soda causes acid rebound.
- "Cleansing" remedies have no clinical basis and can worsen symptoms.
- GERD is well-controlled — catastrophic "esophageal cancer" scenarios are usually not justified, but that's precisely why periodic medical review matters, not years of self-medication.
