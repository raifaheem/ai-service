# High Blood Pressure: Lowering It and Keeping It Down

Hypertension is the most preventable risk factor for stroke and heart attack. According to WHO, about 1.3 billion adults worldwide live with elevated blood pressure, and roughly half don't know it yet. The good news: for most patients, a combination of lifestyle changes and — when needed — one or two medications is enough to bring pressure back into a safe range.

## What counts as normal

Current WHO and most clinical-society thresholds:

- **Normal**: below 120/80 mm Hg.
- **Elevated**: 120–129 / under 80.
- **Stage 1 hypertension**: 130–139 / 80–89.
- **Stage 2 hypertension**: 140/90 or above.

Diagnosis isn't made from one reading, but from several on different days at rest. A single high number after a run or at the doctor's office ("white-coat hypertension") isn't a diagnosis.

## How to measure at home properly

- Measure in the morning before coffee, medications, or food; again in the evening before bed.
- Sit with your back supported, feet on the floor, arm on a table at heart level.
- 5 minutes of quiet before the reading.
- Take two readings one minute apart; record the average.
- Use a correctly sized cuff: one that's too narrow reads falsely high.

Keep a log for at least 7 days and show it to your clinician. That gives an honest picture a single office reading can't.

## What works without medication

- **Weight loss.** Losing even 5–10% of body weight lowers pressure by 5–10 mm Hg.
- **Less salt.** Aim for under 5 g (one teaspoon) per day. Main sources are processed foods, deli meats, cheese, bread, and sauces. Simply "salting less at the table" rarely does the job alone.
- **Physical activity.** 150 minutes of moderate aerobic exercise per week plus 2 strength sessions. Pressure drops even at modest exercise levels.
- **Alcohol.** No more than 1 drink per day for women, 2 for men. Many people with hypertension feel better stopping entirely.
- **Smoking.** Every cigarette spikes pressure for 15–30 minutes; quitting is one of the fastest wins.
- **Sleep.** Chronic short sleep and untreated sleep apnea are common causes of stubborn hypertension. Loud snoring with breathing pauses — get tested.

## When to see a doctor

- When home readings stay above 135/85, even when you feel fine.
- When you're already on treatment and targets aren't met within 3 months.
- During dose adjustment — follow-up every 2–4 weeks minimum.
- Any pregnancy — separate protocols apply; pressure above 140/90 requires immediate evaluation.

## Red flags — seek emergency care

Call for emergency care if pressure is above 180/120 and you have any of:

- **Severe headache**, unlike your usual headaches.
- **Chest pain**, shortness of breath, pain radiating to arm or jaw.
- **Slurred speech, arm or leg weakness, facial droop** — possible stroke.
- **Vision changes**, confusion.
- **Nosebleed** that won't stop within 20 minutes.

A hypertensive emergency is not "I'll wait it out" — it's an episode that needs prompt medical assessment.

## What doesn't work

- **IV "vessel cleaning"** drips or "microcirculation boosters" — no effect on blood pressure.
- **Crash low-carb diets** as a standalone measure — short-term only; pressure returns without broader lifestyle changes.
- **Self-stopping medication** when you feel better. Hypertension is chronic; most people need ongoing treatment, with only the dose changing over time.
