# Cardio Basics for Beginners

Aerobic ("cardio") exercise is the type of activity that lowers risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, several cancers, and extends life. The good news: you don't need to run marathons or spend hours in a gym to get a meaningful effect.

## How much movement you need

WHO recommendations for adults 18–64:

- **150–300 minutes per week** of **moderate**-intensity activity, OR
- **75–150 minutes** of **vigorous**-intensity activity, OR
- an equivalent combination of the two,
- plus **2 or more days per week** of muscle-strengthening exercise.

Moderate intensity = brisk walking, easy cycling, swimming at a comfortable pace. At moderate effort you can talk in short phrases but can't sing.

Vigorous intensity = running, fast cycling, energetic dancing, hill climbing. You can only manage single words.

## Why aerobic exercise works

- **Heart** — gets stronger, pumps more blood per beat, resting pulse falls.
- **Vessels** — endothelial function improves, atherosclerosis risk drops.
- **Metabolism** — insulin sensitivity improves, fasting glucose drops.
- **Weight** — helps maintain and lose weight when paired with nutrition.
- **Mood** — steady aerobic exercise reduces anxiety and mild-to-moderate depression.
- **Sleep** — boosts night-sleep quality and depth.

## If you haven't trained for a while

Start small and build gradually. The main risk for beginners isn't injury — it's burning out on an over-ambitious start.

**Weeks 1–2.** 15–20 minutes of walking at a comfortable pace 3–4 times per week. The goal is to build the habit, not to load the cardiovascular system.

**Weeks 3–4.** 20–30 minutes of brisk walking 4–5 times per week. Pace: you can talk but you're slightly short of breath.

**Weeks 5–6.** Add intervals: 2 minutes of brisk walking or light jogging, 2 minutes of normal walking. Repeat 6–8 cycles. Total 30–40 minutes, 3 times per week.

**Weeks 7–8.** Expand to 30–45 minutes of continuous moderate-intensity work 4–5 times per week. That already delivers the full WHO recommendation.

## Choosing an activity

The best cardio is the one you'll actually keep doing. Without that, the most "effective" program on paper doesn't work. Try a few options:

- **Brisk walking** — safest, no gear required, suits most people. Add hills for intensity.
- **Running** — effective, no gym needed, but knee and foot loading takes adaptation. Beginners: alternate walking and running.
- **Cycling** — minimal impact, easy on joints, can be combined with a commute.
- **Swimming** — full-body load, fully joint-friendly. Good when overweight or with back/knee problems.
- **Elliptical, rowing machine** — even load without impact, convenient in a gym.
- **Dance, group classes** — add a social element, boost motivation.

## Before you start: when to check with a clinician

Most healthy adults don't need a special evaluation before starting moderate activity. Talk to a clinician if:

- You're over 45 and haven't trained for many years.
- You have a diagnosed cardiovascular condition, diabetes, or chronic lung disease.
- You experience chest pain or pressure, severe shortness of breath on minimal effort, or fainting episodes.
- You have a medical condition that limits exertion.

## Monitoring intensity

Two simple ways to assess intensity:

- **The "talk test"** — at moderate intensity you speak in phrases, at vigorous in single words.
- **Heart rate** — target zone for moderate effort: 50–70% of max. Max is roughly 220 − age. For a 40-year-old, that's 90–126 bpm.

A heart-rate monitor or fitness watch isn't required but makes the load more intentional. For a healthy person, going by how you feel is enough.

## What helps you stick with it

- **Consistency over intensity.** Four 30-minute sessions beat one 2-hour session.
- **Plan ahead.** Put workouts on your calendar like meetings.
- **Change into sportswear early.** A physical cue lowers the barrier to entry.
- **Start even when you don't feel like it.** The "10-minute rule" — tell yourself you'll only train for 10 minutes. 90% of the time you continue.
- **Keep a simple log** — date, type of activity, duration. Visible progress motivates.
- **Rotate activities** — reduces overload on the same muscles and keeps interest fresh.

## Signs you should ease off

- Joint pain lasting more than 24 hours after training.
- Mounting fatigue, worse sleep, irritability.
- Frequent colds — possible overtraining sign.
- Resting heart rate elevated 10+ beats above your normal.

3–7 days of light activity or rest typically restore balance.
