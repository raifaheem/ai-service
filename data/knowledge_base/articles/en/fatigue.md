# Chronic Fatigue: What to Look For

Fatigue that doesn't improve after a weekend, a full night's sleep, or a vacation is not just "laziness" — it's a medical symptom that deserves attention.

## Physiological vs. pathological fatigue

Temporary tiredness after an intense week, during jet lag, or while fighting off a cold is a normal response that resolves in 1–3 days.

**Chronic** fatigue lasts more than 4 weeks, persists even after rest, and interferes with normal activities. The causes generally fall into three groups: medical, psychological, and lifestyle.

## Medical causes worth ruling out

- **Iron deficiency anemia** — especially in women of reproductive age. Key tests: complete blood count, ferritin.
- **Vitamin B12 deficiency** — common in older adults, vegans, people after gastric surgery.
- **Thyroid dysfunction** — hypothyroidism causes fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance. Basic screen: TSH.
- **Type 2 diabetes** — particularly if fatigue is combined with thirst and frequent urination.
- **Sleep apnea** — if you snore loudly and wake up tired despite 8 hours in bed. A bed partner may notice breathing pauses.
- **Chronic inflammatory conditions** and viral infections (including post-COVID syndrome).
- **Medication side effects** — some antihistamines, beta-blockers, muscle relaxants, antidepressants.

## Psychological causes

Depression and chronic stress are among the leading causes of persistent fatigue. If along with exhaustion you notice reduced interest in usual activities, low mood most of the day for more than 2 weeks, sleep disturbances, or appetite changes — discuss it with a doctor.

"Burnout" is a distinct phenomenon characterized by emotional exhaustion, cynicism, and reduced productivity. Treatment starts not with pills but with changes to work conditions, boundaries, and recovery time.

## Lifestyle contributors

Often fatigue is a direct result of how the day is structured:

- **Insufficient sleep** — most adults need 7–9 hours. Regularly getting less than 6 hours is a risk factor not only for fatigue but also for cardiovascular disease.
- **Irregular sleep schedule** with more than a 1–2 hour difference between weekday and weekend wake times ("social jet lag").
- **Sedentary lifestyle** — paradoxically, 30 minutes of brisk walking per day reduces fatigue better than an extra hour on the couch.
- **Excess caffeine** after lunch disrupts night sleep architecture.
- **Alcohol** — even one or two drinks in the evening impairs deep sleep.
- **Low-calorie diets**, skipping breakfast, chronic dehydration.

## A practical algorithm

If you've had persistent fatigue for more than a month:

1. **Sleep** — fixed bedtime and wake time, 7–9 hours. Dark, cool bedroom. No screens for an hour before bed.
2. **Movement** — 30 minutes of moderate activity daily. Start with walking.
3. **Nutrition** — regular meals, adequate protein (1 g per kg body weight), vegetables, whole grains. Limit refined sugar.
4. **Hydration** — 30–35 ml of water per kg of body weight per day.
5. **Doctor's visit** — a basic workup (CBC, ferritin, TSH, glucose, vitamin B12) is inexpensive and informative.
6. **Mood screening** — a simple PHQ-9 questionnaire you can complete on your own; discuss results with a doctor.

## Red flags

Seek urgent medical care if fatigue is accompanied by:

- Unexplained weight loss of more than 5% over 1–2 months.
- Night sweats or fever.
- Enlarged lymph nodes, shortness of breath, leg swelling.
- Bleeding (nose, gums, in stool).

Fatigue is a real symptom, and for most people there's a treatable cause. Don't write it off as "age" or "character."
