# Stress: How to Recognize It and What to Do

Stress isn't necessarily bad. Short, surmountable stress (exam prep, a deadline, a presentation) mobilizes you and helps you act. Chronic stress — when the body lives for years in heightened-alarm mode — damages health slowly but systemically.

## What happens in the body

Stress activates the hypothalamus–pituitary–adrenal axis. Cortisol and adrenaline are released, heart rate goes up, blood pressure rises, muscles tense, digestion slows. In the short term this is useful. If the state persists for weeks and months without recovery, consequences build up:

- Sleep disturbance, chronic fatigue.
- Tension headaches and muscle pain, especially neck and shoulders.
- GI issues — heartburn, irritable bowel, loss of appetite or overeating.
- Elevated blood pressure, faster heart rate, increased cardiovascular event risk.
- Worse concentration, forgetfulness.
- Reduced immunity — more frequent colds.
- Anxiety, irritability, apathy, emotional burnout.

## How to recognize it

Stress symptoms are easy to confuse with "bad mood" or fatigue. Pay attention to the combination:

- **Emotional:** irritability without reason, sense of overload, anxiety, apathy, hard to enjoy ordinary things.
- **Physical:** persistent fatigue, sleep problems, headaches, muscle tension, GI disturbance.
- **Cognitive:** trouble concentrating, forgetting things you used to remember, harder to make decisions.
- **Behavioral:** more alcohol, coffee, sugar; procrastination; social withdrawal.

If three or more of these signs persist for 2–4 weeks — it's time to act.

## What helps, with evidence

Managing stress isn't a single pill but a set of habits that work together.

### 1. Sleep

Sleep deprivation by itself raises cortisol. 7–9 hours of regular sleep is the baseline condition of stress resilience. See the insomnia article for details.

### 2. Physical activity

30 minutes of moderate activity per day is one of the strongest anti-stress tools available. Aerobic exercise lowers cortisol and boosts endorphin production. Yoga and Pilates add a conscious-breathing component.

### 3. Breathing techniques

The fastest way to activate the parasympathetic nervous system and reduce arousal:

- **4-6 breathing:** 4-second nasal inhale, 6-second mouth exhale. The extended exhale triggers relaxation. 5 minutes of practice already produces an effect.
- **Box breathing:** inhale 4 sec, hold 4 sec, exhale 4 sec, hold 4 sec.
- **Diaphragmatic breathing:** place a hand on your belly, breathe so the belly rises while the chest stays nearly still.

### 4. Mindfulness and meditation

10–20 minutes of daily meditation reduces anxiety, improves concentration and sleep quality. Simple apps and guided body-scan or breath-observation sessions are good starters.

### 5. Structured rest

Rest isn't the same as lying on the couch. What works:

- A 20–30-minute nature walk without your phone.
- Distraction-free time with people who matter to you.
- A hobby where you find "flow" — music, drawing, fixing things, sport.
- A daily schedule with a fixed end-of-work time.

### 6. Nutrition

Regular meals stabilize blood glucose, and that stabilizes mood. Limit excess caffeine (no more than 400 mg, or about 4 cups of coffee per day; last one before 2 p.m.). Alcohol gives short-term relaxation but worsens sleep and increases anxiety the next day.

### 7. Social support

Talking to a close person isn't "weakness" — it's a biologically built-in mechanism. Even a 15-minute meet-up with a friend reduces cortisol. Loneliness, conversely, raises it.

### 8. Planning

Many stresses are linked to the feeling that "everything is piling up". What helps:

- Write down all tasks on paper (off-loads cognitive load).
- Sort by "important/urgent" (Eisenhower matrix).
- Plan 3 key tasks for the day, not 15.

## When to see a doctor or psychologist

Seek help if:

- Stress has lasted several months and self-help isn't moving the needle.
- Pronounced symptoms of anxiety or depression: constant worry, panic attacks, loss of interest, thoughts of self-harm.
- Work and personal life are suffering.
- Alcohol or other psychoactive substance use has increased.
- Physical symptoms (chest pain, blood-pressure spikes, weight loss) need clinical evaluation.

Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT), group stress-management programs, and in some cases medication are effective and safe. This isn't "weakness" or "for life": a course is typically 10–20 sessions.

## What doesn't work, or works worse

- **Self-medicating with alcohol.** Gives 1–2 hours of illusory relief, then makes sleep, mood, and anxiety worse.
- **"Switching off" on weekends.** A single long rest doesn't compensate for chronic stress; daily micro-pauses do.
- **"Anti-stress" supplements** without evidence. Magnesium and vitamins only work in a confirmed deficiency, which a test needs to verify.
