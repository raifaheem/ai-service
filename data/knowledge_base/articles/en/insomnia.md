# Insomnia: What Helps Without Sleeping Pills

Insomnia is diagnosed when a person has difficulty falling asleep, wakes up frequently at night, or wakes very early — and this happens at least three nights a week for three months. Brief episodes of poor sleep during stress or illness are normal and resolve on their own.

## Why insomnia becomes self-sustaining

Insomnia often starts as a reaction to a specific event — a worry, a trip, a job change — and then persists because of learned habits: going to bed earlier "just in case", lying awake for hours, worrying about "not being able to sleep again", drinking more coffee during the day to cope. All of this turns the bed into a place of anxiety rather than sleep and maintains the cycle.

## First-line treatment is not medication

International guidelines (CDC, NHS, AASM) rank **cognitive-behavioral therapy for insomnia** (CBT-I) as the first choice. It's not "talking about feelings" but a structured 6–8 session program. When CBT-I isn't available, most of its components can be applied independently.

## Sleep hygiene principles

- **Consistent wake time** — the most powerful anchor of circadian rhythm. Get up at the same time every day, even on weekends, even after a bad night.
- **Bed is for sleep only** (and sex). Don't work, eat, or watch shows in bed. This restores the "bed = sleep" association.
- **If you can't fall asleep in 20 minutes, get up.** Go to another room, do something boring in dim light (a paper book, simple handiwork). Return to bed only when you feel sleepy.
- **Naps** limited to 20–30 minutes, before 3 PM.
- **Caffeine** — last cup no later than 8 hours before bed. Caffeine has a 5–6 hour half-life.
- **Alcohol** helps with falling asleep but disrupts sleep architecture in the second half of the night. Minimize evening drinks.
- **Nicotine** is a stimulant — smoking before bed worsens sleep onset.
- **Physical activity** of 30 minutes per day improves sleep, but finish intense workouts 3 hours before bedtime.

## Bedroom setup

- Dark, **cool (18–20 °C / 65–68 °F), quiet** bedroom.
- Blackout curtains or an eye mask.
- Electronic devices out of the bedroom or on "do not disturb".
- Dim bright and blue light an hour before bed.

## Sleep restriction technique

A counterintuitive but one of the most effective CBT-I tools:

1. Determine how much you actually sleep on average (using a sleep diary for 1–2 weeks). For example: 5 hours.
2. Spend exactly that much time in bed — not a minute more. If you get up at 7 AM and sleep 5 hours, go to bed at 2 AM.
3. When 90% of that time is spent asleep, move bedtime earlier by 15 minutes.

The first week is tough due to daytime sleepiness, but over 2–4 weeks sleep usually consolidates.

## Relaxation techniques before bed

- **Diaphragmatic breathing**: 4 seconds in through the nose, 6 seconds out through the mouth, for 5–10 minutes.
- **Progressive muscle relaxation**: sequentially tense and relax muscle groups from feet to face.
- **"Brain dump"**: 30 minutes before bed, write down worries and tomorrow's tasks. This reduces nighttime rumination.

## Sleeping pills: what to know

- Prescription sleep aids (z-drugs, benzodiazepines) can help in acute situations as a short 1–2 week course, but are not for long-term use — they cause dependence and worsen sleep quality over time.
- Over-the-counter "sleep aids" based on diphenhydramine cause drowsiness but are poorly tolerated by older adults (confusion, falls).
- Melatonin is more effective for circadian rhythm disorders (jet lag, shift work) than for classic insomnia.
- Any sleeping pill works worse than behavioral measures in the long term.

## When to see a doctor

See a specialist if:

- Insomnia lasts more than three months despite sleep hygiene.
- You fall asleep unintentionally during the day (driving, in meetings) — this may indicate sleep apnea or narcolepsy.
- Your partner notices loud snoring and breathing pauses.
- Insomnia is accompanied by low mood, anxiety, or panic attacks.
