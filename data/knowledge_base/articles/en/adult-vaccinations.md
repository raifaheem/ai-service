# Adult Vaccinations: What You Actually Need, and When

Vaccination isn't just a childhood topic. Immunity wanes with age, new pathogens emerge, and some vaccines meaningfully reduce the risk of severe illness and complications in adults. WHO and national immunization bodies revise recommendations every 1–2 years; what follows is a general guide — your individual schedule is best confirmed with a clinician.

## Relevant to most adults

- **Tetanus and diphtheria** (Td or Tdap with a pertussis component). Booster every 10 years. After a skin-breaking injury, check when your last dose was — if it's been more than 5 years, an earlier booster may be recommended.
- **Influenza.** Annually, ideally in autumn before the season starts. Especially important for adults 65+, people with chronic conditions (diabetes, COPD, heart failure), and healthcare workers.
- **COVID-19.** Recommendations keep evolving; in general, periodic boosters on a defined schedule, especially for at-risk groups. Follow current WHO and local health-authority guidance.
- **MMR (measles, mumps, rubella).** If you didn't receive two documented childhood doses, get vaccinated — especially before travel to regions with active outbreaks.
- **Hepatitis B.** 3-dose series. Recommended for all unvaccinated adults up to 59; older adults by indication (diabetes, medical procedures, healthcare contact).

## Age- and condition-specific

- **Shingles (zoster).** From age 50 — recombinant vaccine, 2 doses. Reduces severe shingles and postherpetic neuralgia risk by over 90%.
- **Pneumococcal.** After 65, and earlier with chronic conditions. The schedule depends on prior doses — your clinician calculates it.
- **RSV** (respiratory syncytial virus). Newly recommended for adults 60+ — ask your clinician for the current schedule.
- **Hepatitis A.** By indication: travel, chronic liver disease, occupational exposure.

## Situational vaccines

- **Travel.** Yellow fever, typhoid, Japanese encephalitis, rabies — depending on destination. Plan 4–6 weeks before departure.
- **Animal contact**, healthcare, lab work — separate protocols apply.

## Common questions

- **"I already had the illness — why the vaccine?"** Natural immunity is strong after some infections, but wanes quickly after pertussis, influenza, and COVID-19. Vaccination adds protection.
- **"Are several shots at once harmful?"** The immune system meets millions of antigens daily. Combining vaccines is a safe, routine practice shown in dozens of studies.
- **"I feel worse after vaccination."** A normal response to most vaccines is 1–2 days of mild malaise and local soreness. That's the immune system working. Serious reactions are rare and evaluable.

## When clinician sign-off is required

An individualized plan is mandatory with:

- Immunosuppression (chemotherapy, transplant, systemic corticosteroids, biologics).
- Active autoimmune disease.
- Allergic reactions to prior vaccines.
- Recent serious illness or surgery.

## Red flags after vaccination

Seek emergency care for:

- **Anaphylaxis** — within minutes of injection: facial swelling, trouble breathing, widespread rash, sudden drop in blood pressure. Vaccination rooms keep epinephrine on hand.
- **High fever with widespread rash**, trouble breathing within the first 24 hours.
- **Severe pain and swelling extending beyond the shoulder**, not improving by 72 hours.
